# 02 義務論 / Deontology

Deontology (from Greek *deon*, "duty") holds that the rightness of actions is determined
by duties and rules, not solely by consequences. 義務論主張行為的對錯取決於義務與規則，
而不僅僅是後果。

## 核心原則 / Core Principle

> "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should
> become a universal law." — Immanuel Kant, *Groundwork* (1785)

## 康德的定言令式 / Kant's Categorical Imperative

Kant distinguishes between **hypothetical imperatives 假言令式** (if you want X, do Y)
and the **categorical imperative 定言令式** (do Y, unconditionally).
Moral duties arise from the categorical imperative alone.

### 三大表述 / Three Formulations

| # | Formulation | English | 中文 | Core Idea |
|:--|:------------|:--------|:-----|:----------|
| **F1** | Formula of Universal Law | 普遍法則公式 | "Act only on that maxim which you can will as universal law" | Consistency: can my maxim be universalized without contradiction? |
| **F2** | Formula of Humanity | 人性公式 | "Act so that you treat humanity, whether in yourself or another, always as an end, never merely as a means" | Respect: persons have dignity, not price |
| **F3** | Formula of the Kingdom of Ends | 目的王國公式 | "Act as if you were a legislating member of the kingdom of ends" | Autonomy: we are both authors and subjects of moral law |

### 矛盾類型 / Types of Contradiction

When testing a maxim (F1), Kant identifies two kinds of failure:

1. **Contradiction in Conception 概念矛盾**: The maxim cannot even be conceived as universal law
   - *Example*: False promising — if everyone made false promises, the institution of promising would collapse
2. **Contradiction in the Will 意志矛盾**: The maxim can be conceived as universal but cannot rationally be willed
   - *Example*: Refusing to help others — you could conceive a world where no one helps anyone,
     but as a rational being you cannot *will* it (you might need help yourself)

### 經典案例 / Classic Cases

| Case | Maxim | Universalizable? | Treats as End? | Kantian Verdict |
|:-----|:------|:----------------:|:--------------:|:----------------|
| Lying to save an innocent life | "Lie when it saves lives" | No (contradiction in conception) | No (uses listener as mere means) | **Wrong** |
| Suicide from despair | "End life to escape suffering" | No (contradiction in conception) | Violates humanity in oneself | **Wrong** |
| Neglecting one's talents | "Let talents rust" | No (contradiction in will) | Fails to respect humanity | **Wrong** |
| Refusing charity | "Never help others" | No (contradiction in will) | — | **Wrong** |

## 斧頭殺手反駁 / The Axe Murderer Objection

**Scenario**: A would-be murderer knocks on your door asking where your friend (the intended
victim) is hiding. If you tell the truth, your friend dies. Should you lie?

**Kant's (alleged) answer**: No. Lying is always wrong. Kant argues in "On a Supposed Right
to Lie from Philanthropic Motives" (1797) that even lying to a murderer violates the
categorical imperative.

**Responses 回應**:
- **Korsgaard**: We can use the Formula of Humanity — the murderer is not a rational agent
  in that context, so we aren't treating him as an end
- **Ross**: *Prima facie* duty of non-maleficence can override the duty of veracity
- **Consequentialist**: This shows deontology is absurd — the consequences clearly matter!

## 羅斯的表面義務 / W.D. Ross's Prima Facie Duties

Ross (*The Right and the Good*, 1930) offers a pluralist deontology:

**Seven Prima Facie Duties 七項表面義務**:
1. **Fidelity 忠誠**: Keep promises
2. **Reparation 補償**: Make amends for wrongs done
3. **Gratitude 感恩**: Return favors
4. **Justice 正義**: Distribute goods fairly
5. **Beneficence 慈善**: Help others
6. **Non-maleficence 不傷害**: Avoid harming others
7. **Self-improvement 自我完善**: Develop one's own virtues

**Key Insight**: Duties can conflict. We must use judgment (*phronesis*-adjacent) to determine
which duty is most stringent in a given situation. No master rule resolves all conflicts.

## 比較：義務論 vs 功利主義 / Deontology vs Utilitarianism

| Issue | Deontology | Utilitarianism |
|:------|:-----------|:---------------|
| **Locus of value** | Persons, dignity, rational nature | Pleasure, preference-satisfaction, well-being |
| **Constraints** | Side-constraints: do not violate rights even for good outcomes | No constraints: any action justified if net benefit |
| **Intention** | What matters is the maxim/intention | What matters is the outcome |
| **The Trolley Problem** | Push = wrong (using person as mere means); Switch = possibly permissible | Both permissible if they save more lives |

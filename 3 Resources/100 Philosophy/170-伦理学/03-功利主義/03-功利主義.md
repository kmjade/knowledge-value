# 03 功利主義 / Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism is the consequentialist theory that the right action is the one that maximizes
overall well-being — the "greatest happiness for the greatest number."
功利主義主張正確的行為是最大化整體福祉的行為。

## 核心原則 / Core Principle

> "Nature has placed mankind under the governance of two sovereign masters, pain and pleasure.
> It is for them alone to point out what we ought to do." — Jeremy Bentham, *Principles of Morals and Legislation* (1789)

## 邊沁的快樂計算 / Bentham's Hedonic Calculus

Bentham proposed a quantitative method for moral decision-making — the **felicific calculus
快樂計算** — measuring pleasure along seven dimensions:

| Dimension | English | Description |
|:----------|:--------|:------------|
| Intensity | 強度 | How strong is the pleasure/pain? |
| Duration | 持續時間 | How long does it last? |
| Certainty | 確定性 | How likely is the outcome? |
| Propinquity | 遠近性 | How soon will it occur? |
| Fecundity | 衍生性 | Will it produce more pleasure/pain? |
| Purity | 純度 | Is it mixed with the opposite sensation? |
| Extent | 範圍 | How many people are affected? |

**Criticism**: Bentham's calculus treats all pleasures as equal — "pushpin is as good as poetry."

## 穆勒的改進 / Mill's Higher Pleasures

John Stuart Mill (*Utilitarianism*, 1861) responded to the "pig philosophy" critique:

> "It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates
> dissatisfied than a fool satisfied."

**Higher vs Lower Pleasures 高級與低級快樂**:
- **Higher pleasures 高級快樂**: Intellectual, moral, aesthetic — uniquely human capacities
- **Lower pleasures 低級快樂**: Sensory, bodily pleasures — shared with animals
- **Competent judges test 勝任裁判者測試**: Those who have experienced both prefer the higher

### Mill's Harm Principle 傷害原則

> "The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized
> community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others." — *On Liberty* (1859)

## 行為 vs 規則功利主義 / Act vs Rule Utilitarianism

| Type | Principle | Key Thinker | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|:-----|:----------|:------------|:----------|:-------------|
| **Act Utilitarianism 行為功利** | Each act judged by its consequences | Bentham | Flexible, case-sensitive | Justifies rights violations; too demanding |
| **Rule Utilitarianism 規則功利** | Follow rules whose general adoption maximizes welfare | Mill, Brandt, Harsanyi | Protects rights; more practical | Collapses into act utilitarianism? Rule worship? |

## 經典反對意見 / Classic Objections

### 1. The Utility Monster 功利怪物 (Nozick, *Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, 1974)
Imagine a being who gains vastly more utility from resources than anyone else. Utilitarianism
would demand giving everything to this monster — even at the cost of immense suffering to others.
**Response**: Diminishing marginal utility — real beings don't have infinite capacity for utility.

### 2. The Experience Machine 經驗機器 (Nozick, 1974)
If we only value experiences, we should plug into a machine that simulates a perfect life.
Nozick argues we wouldn't — we value reality, autonomy, and genuine connection.
**Implication**: Well-being is not just about subjective experience. Preference utilitarianism
(Hare, Singer) captures this: we prefer real achievements, not just the experience of them.

### 3. The Demandingness Objection 過度要求反駁
Utilitarianism seems to require us to give away nearly all our resources to the global poor.
**Response**: (a) So what? Maybe morality *is* demanding. (b) Rule utilitarianism permits
some agent-centered prerogatives. (c) Effective altruism embraces the demandingness.

### 4. Justice and Rights 正義與權利
Utilitarianism could justify punishing the innocent, slavery, or violating rights if it
produces net benefits.
**Response**: Rule utilitarianism probably forbids such practices. And in practice, such
actions rarely maximize utility long-term.

## 彼得·辛格與有效利他主義 / Singer & Effective Altruism

### Singer's Drowning Child Argument 溺水兒童論證
If you walked past a child drowning in a shallow pond, you would ruin your clothes to save
them. The global poor are dying from preventable causes. Distance and numbers don't matter
morally. Therefore, we have an obligation to donate significantly.

### Effective Altruism (EA) 有效利他主義
A social movement applying utilitarian reasoning to maximize the good per dollar donated:
- **GiveWell**: Evidence-based charity evaluation
- **80,000 Hours**: Career choice for maximum impact
- **Longtermism**: Future generations matter; existential risk reduction may be the highest-impact cause

| EA Principle | Description |
|:-------------|:------------|
| **Impartiality 平等** | All lives count equally regardless of distance, species, or time |
| **Cause prioritization 原因優先排序** | Choose the most tractable, neglected, and large-scale problems |
| **Evidence-based 證據導向** | Use data, not intuition, to guide giving |

## 比較表 / Comparison with Rival Theories

| Criterion | Utilitarianism | Deontology | Virtue Ethics |
|:----------|:--------------:|:----------:|:-------------:|
| What matters | Consequences | Duties / Rules | Character |
| Impartiality | Maximally impartial | Respects agent-relative reasons | Virtues are agent-relative |
| Action guidance | Strong (calculus) | Strong (CI test) | Weak (too vague?) |
| Treatment of minorities | Potentially sacrifices | Protects via side-constraints | Protects via virtue of justice |
| Demandingness | Very demanding | Moderately demanding | Moderately demanding |
